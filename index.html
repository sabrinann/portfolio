Taking a SaaS Platform from 0→1

Designed and launched the first module of a modular pharmaceutical field enablement platform, taking it from concept to first paying client within my first year in Product.

01The Problem

Pharmaceutical HQ teams invest heavily in commercial strategy, but execution breaks down in the field. Reps receive fragmented content, messaging varies across territories, and there's no centralized platform for HQ to author, govern, and distribute content consistently.

02Strategic Approach

I chose a modular platform architecture to serve two objectives: faster time-to-market (ship the first module, validate, then build) and flexible commercialization (clients subscribe only to modules they need). I prioritized content authoring and deployment as V1, the highest immediate value and most commercially viable entry point, while designing for a broader HQ-to-field workflow over time.

03Key Decisions
  • Built five independently subscribable modules, each delivering standalone value
  • Sequenced content authoring first to maximize commercial traction
  • Led product and delivery: stood up a scrum team from scratch, maintained tight UX feedback loops, navigated a container migration mid-build
04Impact

Went from zero to a live, commercially available platform module within one year, with a paying client signed within days of launch

Modular architecture meant each subsequent module could be scoped, priced, and shipped independently, with no rewrites and no dependency bottlenecks

Established the product and delivery foundation that the company's entire HQ-to-field platform strategy now builds on

05Reflection

The biggest shift for me was realizing that 0→1 product work doesn't come with clean inputs or clear answers. I had to get comfortable making decisions with incomplete information, but in a structured way: naming assumptions, weighing tradeoffs, and choosing a direction that balanced immediate progress with long-term viability, while staying ready to adjust as new signals emerged.

Scalability Over Customization

Turned a high-pressure enterprise implementation crisis into a platform-level solution designed to scale beyond a single client.

01The Problem

Our first enterprise client exposed a data scalability issue the platform had never encountered. Their account-rep relationships were exponentially more complex than prior clients, and the system strained under volume, but the issue only surfaced in their environment, making diagnosis significantly harder.

02Strategic Decision

Facing overlapping Salesforce API limits, performance thresholds, and shifting client timelines, I had to make a critical call: implement a fast client-specific workaround or redesign the platform architecture. We couldn't fully replicate the issue internally, so I was making the decision with incomplete data. I pushed for the platform-level redesign because, while riskier short-term, it addressed the root cause and would support any future enterprise client rather than just patch this one scenario.

03Execution
  • Worked directly with engineering to diagnose issues in the client environment and isolate root causes across multiple constraint layers
  • Collaborated to redesign data sync, caching strategies, batching logic, and Azure function concurrency
  • Maintained engineering focus on the core technical problem while I managed all stakeholder communication
04Stakeholder Mgmt
  • Served as central coordination point across engineering, QA, client delivery, and client success
  • Communicated timeline adjustments to client and commercial stakeholders with clear reasoning
  • Maintained clarity on what was product scope vs. scope creep as constraints evolved in real time
05Impact

Enterprise client launched successfully: the platform's first deployment at that scale, unblocking a strategically critical commercial relationship

Platform redesign removed the ceiling on enterprise complexity, so future clients at similar scale can onboard without engineering intervention or custom workarounds

System stability improved across all existing clients as a byproduct of the architectural work, not just for the enterprise case

06Reflection

Looking back, a workaround would have been faster, but it would have quietly set the direction for every future client in a way that wouldn't scale. The real work was keeping engineering unblocked while coordinating across QA, client delivery, and Commercial Success under pressure.

Discovery-Driven MVP for an AI-Powered Product

Transformed an unfocused hackathon prototype into a validated, trust-aware AI product blueprint ready for investment.

01The Problem

An AI-powered pre-call planning prototype aimed to help pharma sales reps prepare for HCP engagements, but it was built around the wrong user problem. It combined territory planning (a brand manager problem) with interaction-level prep (a rep problem), and wasn't grounded in how reps actually work.

02The Reframe

I reframed the core question from "Who should I prioritize this week?" to "What do I need to know to make this conversation count?" This shifted the product from territory planning to in-the-moment clarity, solving for the rep's actual pain point and moment of need.

03MVP Strategy

Designed a three-phase sequence to build trust progressively:

  • V1: AI-generated talking points for scheduled visits that augment prep without changing behavior
  • V2: Proximity-based recommendations while in the field, introducing prioritization in a low-risk context
  • V3: Territory-level daily prioritization, introduced only after trust is established
04Validation
  • Conducted VOC interviews with brand managers across multiple pharma companies
  • Engaged data science through office hours to understand AI feasibility constraints
  • Built product narrative grounded in user needs and technical reality
05Impact

Shifted the product away from a problem nobody owned, territory planning, to one reps feel daily, making the concept fundable where the prototype wasn't

Delivered a sequenced MVP blueprint leadership could evaluate and invest in incrementally, rather than an all-or-nothing AI bet

Positioned for 2026 roadmap investment with a rollout plan that builds user trust gradually, reducing the risk of AI rejection at the user level before launch

06Reflection

This experience reinforced that early-stage product work often requires reframing the problem itself, especially when initial solutions are built around the wrong user or moment of need.

The initial concept was technically impressive, but it was solving the wrong problem for the wrong user. Taking the time to step back, challenge the framing, and validate it with real users fundamentally changed the trajectory of the product.
